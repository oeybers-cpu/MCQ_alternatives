Rethinking Assessment in Academic Literacy

The Evolution of Academic Literacy Assessment

For decades, academic literacy has been assessed through standardized multiple-choice questions (MCQs), which prioritize recall over critical engagement. This approach emerged from industrial-era education models that valued efficiency and standardization. However, as academic discourse has evolved toward more complex, multimodal forms of knowledge production, MCQs have become increasingly inadequate for measuring true academic literacy.

True academic literacy encompasses the ability to navigate disciplinary conventions, engage with complex texts, construct evidence-based arguments, and communicate effectively across multiple modalities. MCQs reduce this rich complexity to binary choices, failing to capture the depth of understanding required in contemporary academic environments.

Academic literacy cannot be reduced to discrete factual recall; it is a situated practice that requires engagement with context, purpose, and audience.
Understanding the historical context of academic literacy assessment

The Scholarly Critique of MCQ Assessments

Academic scholarship has consistently highlighted the limitations of MCQs for assessing academic literacy. Researchers note that MCQs primarily measure surface-level knowledge retention rather than deep understanding or critical thinking. They fail to capture the nuanced reasoning processes that characterize academic work, instead rewarding test-taking strategies over genuine comprehension.

Studies show that MCQs disproportionately disadvantage students from diverse backgrounds who may have different approaches to knowledge construction. The format privileges certain cognitive styles while marginalizing others, reinforcing inequities in educational assessment. Furthermore, MCQs provide minimal feedback for learning, offering only correct/incorrect responses without guidance for improvement.

Standardized testing frameworks often measure what is easiest to test rather than what is most important for academic success.
Evidence-based critique of MCQ limitations in academic contexts

GenAI: Expanding Assessment Possibilities

Generative AI offers transformative potential for academic literacy assessment by enabling multimodal evaluation that mirrors authentic academic practice. Unlike static MCQs, GenAI can analyze complex written arguments, interpret visual data, evaluate multimodal presentations, and provide nuanced feedback across diverse communication forms. This technology supports assessment that reflects how knowledge is actually produced and communicated in contemporary academic environments.

GenAI tools can assess the coherence of arguments, the quality of evidence, the appropriateness of disciplinary conventions, and the effectiveness of communication across multiple modalities. They can evaluate how students synthesize information from diverse sources, construct original arguments, and adapt their communication to specific academic contexts—capabilities far beyond what MCQs can measure.

GenAI assessment moves beyond measuring what students know to evaluating how they think, create, and communicate within academic discourse.
How generative AI expands assessment capabilities for academic literacy

Formative and Dialogic Assessment with GenAI

Traditional MCQ assessments are summative by nature, offering little opportunity for learning during the evaluation process. In contrast, GenAI enables formative and dialogic assessment where evaluation becomes an integral part of the learning journey. Students can engage in iterative cycles of submission, feedback, and revision, with AI providing immediate, context-specific guidance that helps them refine their academic thinking and expression.

This dialogic approach transforms assessment from a judgmental exercise to a collaborative learning process. GenAI can ask probing questions, suggest alternative perspectives, and highlight connections between concepts—fostering the critical engagement that defines academic literacy. Rather than simply testing knowledge, it helps students develop the metacognitive skills necessary for scholarly work.

Assessment should not be an endpoint but a continuous dialogue that guides students toward deeper understanding and more sophisticated academic practice.
Transforming assessment into an ongoing learning process

Authentic Assessment Through GenAI

MCQ assessments often measure artificial constructs disconnected from real academic work. GenAI enables authentic assessment that mirrors the complex, multifaceted nature of scholarly practice. Students can create multimodal projects, engage in simulated academic discourse, and demonstrate their understanding through varied formats that reflect actual disciplinary conventions.

With GenAI, assessment can incorporate real-world academic challenges such as synthesizing information from multiple sources, adapting communication to different audiences, and navigating disciplinary debates. This approach assesses what truly matters in academic literacy: the ability to participate meaningfully in scholarly communities, not just recall isolated facts.

Authentic assessment reflects the complexity of academic work rather than reducing it to discrete, testable elements.
Creating assessment that mirrors real academic practice

Cultivating Evaluative Judgment

MCQs provide no opportunity for students to develop evaluative judgment—the ability to critically assess quality, relevance, and credibility of information and arguments. GenAI, however, can facilitate assessment that develops this crucial academic skill. Students can interact with AI-generated content, evaluate its strengths and weaknesses, and refine their own work based on these evaluations.

Through structured interactions with GenAI tools, students learn to identify logical fallacies, assess evidence quality, recognize bias, and evaluate the coherence of arguments. This metacognitive development is essential for academic literacy, as it prepares students to navigate the complex information landscape of scholarly discourse.

True academic literacy requires not just knowledge acquisition but the ability to critically evaluate knowledge claims and scholarly contributions.
Developing critical evaluation skills through GenAI assessment

The Future of Academic Literacy Assessment

MCQ assessments are fundamentally inadequate for measuring true academic literacy. They reduce complex, nuanced understanding to simplistic binary choices, ignoring the ability to synthesize information, construct arguments, and communicate effectively across multiple modalities. In contrast, multimodal assessment captures the true essence of academic work—where students engage with diverse forms of expression and critical analysis. This is not just an improvement; it's a necessary evolution to foster genuine scholarly competence and prepare students for the complexities of modern academia.

However, this transformation requires careful implementation. Educators must maintain oversight of AI-generated assessments to ensure academic integrity, address algorithmic bias, and preserve human judgment in the evaluation process. The goal is not to replace human assessment but to augment it with technology that expands our capacity to measure what truly matters in academic literacy.

The future of academic literacy assessment lies in technology that enhances human judgment rather than replaces it, creating evaluation frameworks that reflect the richness of scholarly practice.
